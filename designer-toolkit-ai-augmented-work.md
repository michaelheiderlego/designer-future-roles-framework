## The Three Zones

### 🔴 HUMAN CORE (Never delegate)
**What:** The irreplaceable human judgment that defines quality design
**Why:** These require moral reasoning, cultural context, and lived experience
**How to strengthen:**
- Regular critique sessions with diverse perspectives
- Study ethics case studies from tech industry
- Practice articulating "why" not just "what"
- Build decision frameworks you can defend

### 🟠 HUMAN CHECKPOINTS (Direct & approve)
**What:** Critical decision gates where designers intervene in AI workflows
**Why:** AI can generate options, but humans must judge quality, ethics, and fit
**How to work:**
- Set clear criteria before AI generates
- Review with fresh eyes (don't rubber-stamp)
- Document your reasoning for future reference
- Iterate based on checkpoint insights

### 🔵 AI-AUGMENTED ZONE (Orchestrate & curate)
**What:** Tasks where AI assists or automates with human oversight
**Why:** Frees designer time for higher-value judgment work
**How to approach:**
- Learn to brief AI like you'd brief a junior designer
- Curate outputs, don't accept first result
- Maintain quality standards
- Know when to take manual control

## Core Skills Development

### Moral Compass & Judgment
**What it means:**
- Deciding what's "good for humans" not just "good for business"
- Recognizing when convenience becomes manipulation
- Balancing stakeholder needs with user wellbeing

**How to develop:**
- **Practice:** Run ethical impact reviews on existing products
- **Study:** Read "Ruined by Design" (Mike Monteiro), "Design Justice" (Sasha Costanza-Chock)
- **Exercise:** For each feature, ask "What behavior does this encourage?"
- **Collaborate:** Partner with ethicists, social scientists, accessibility experts

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Dark patterns in UI
- Addictive interaction loops
- Exclusionary design decisions
- Privacy-eroding features

---

### Envisioning Desired Futures
**What it means:**
- Looking beyond current data patterns to imagine better possibilities
- Thinking systemically about long-term impact
- Designing for the world we want, not just the world we have

**How to develop:**
- **Practice:** Scenario planning workshops (2030 vision exercises)
- **Study:** Speculative design, futures studies, systems thinking
- **Exercise:** "Future back" thinking - start with desired outcome, work backwards
- **Tools:** Vision boards, journey maps, storyboards of future states

**Questions to ask:**
- What does success look like in 5 years?
- What second-order effects could this create?
- Are we solving symptoms or root causes?
- What would this enable that doesn't exist today?

---

### Creating Simplicity from Chaos
**What it means:**
- Distilling complex problems into clear, elegant solutions
- Saying "no" to feature bloat
- Finding the essential 20% that delivers 80% of value

**How to develop:**
- **Practice:** Constraint-based design challenges (design with only 3 UI elements)
- **Study:** Dieter Rams' principles, minimalism in design
- **Exercise:** For every feature added, remove two
- **Tools:** Priority matrices, value/complexity grids, ruthless editing

**Mantras:**
- "Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away" - Saint-Exupéry
- If you're explaining, you're losing
- Complexity is easy, simplicity is hard

---

### Bridge to Human Experience
**What it means:**
- Translating technical capabilities into human benefits
- Advocating for end-user needs in technical discussions
- Making abstract concepts tangible through storytelling

**How to develop:**
- **Practice:** Regular user research, ride-alongs, support ticket review
- **Study:** Anthropology, storytelling, empathy mapping
- **Exercise:** Translate every technical feature into "so the user can..."
- **Tools:** Personas, journey maps, story frameworks

**Communication techniques:**
- Use concrete examples over abstract concepts
- Show, don't tell (prototypes > specifications)
- Bring user voice into every meeting
- Frame in terms of human emotions and outcomes

---

### The Courage to Say "No"
**What it means:**
- Defending good design against pressure to compromise
- Challenging assumptions and requirements
- Knowing when "not now" is the right answer

**How to develop:**
- **Practice:** Stakeholder negotiation, building credibility through rationale
- **Study:** Influence without authority, principled negotiation
- **Exercise:** Role-play difficult conversations, prepare "no because..." frameworks
- **Support:** Build alliances, document patterns, escalate strategically

**When to say no:**
- Feature requests that contradict core principles
- Dark patterns or manipulative design
- Scope creep without resource adjustment
- "Just add..." without removing anything
- Solutions looking for problems

**How to say no effectively:**
- Lead with curiosity: "Help me understand why..."
- Offer alternatives: "What if instead we..."
- Show tradeoffs: "We could do that, but it means sacrificing..."
- Use data: "Testing showed users struggle when..."
- Invite collaboration: "Let's solve the underlying problem..."

## Checkpoint Tools & Practices

### Checkpoint 1: Problem Definition & Scope
**Your role:** Ensure we're solving the right problem before generating solutions

**Tools:**
- **Problem framing workshops:** "How might we..." statements
- **5 Whys:** Dig beneath surface symptoms to root causes
- **Scope boundaries:** What's in, what's out, what's later
- **Success criteria:** Define "done" and "good" upfront

**AI can help with:**
- Research synthesis from user feedback
- Pattern identification across data sources
- Generating problem reframes

**You must do:**
- Validate that AI hasn't missed context
- Push back on assumed constraints
- Ensure problem is worth solving

**Questions to ask:**
- Are we solving symptoms or root causes?
- Who benefits from this solution? Who might be harmed?
- What happens if we do nothing?
- Is this the simplest problem statement?

---

### Checkpoint 2: Concept Direction & Principles
**Your role:** Set design direction and guardrails before detailed execution

**Tools:**
- **Design principles:** 3-5 guiding statements for this project
- **Inspiration boards:** Visual direction, not literal templates
- **Concept sketches:** Quick, low-fidelity direction setting
- **Design criteria:** What makes a solution "good" for this problem?

**AI can help with:**
- Generating concept variations
- Visual style exploration
- Competitive analysis

**You must do:**
- Choose the strategic direction (AI generates, you decide)
- Ensure concepts align with user needs and brand
- Push concepts beyond obvious first ideas

**Red flags:**
- Concepts feel generic or copied
- Solutions don't match problem scope
- Missing consideration of edge cases
- No clear point of view

---

### Checkpoint 3: Quality Judgment & Refinement
**Your role:** Elevate execution from "functional" to "excellent"

**Tools:**
- **Design critiques:** Structured feedback sessions
- **Quality rubrics:** Specific criteria for excellence
- **Heuristic evaluation:** Check against usability principles
- **A/B testing setup:** Prepare to validate quality claims

**AI can help with:**
- Generating refinement options
- Consistency checking across screens
- Accessibility scans
- Copy variations

**You must do:**
- Judge which refinements truly improve quality
- Maintain design system consistency
- Ensure details support overall intent
- Know when to stop iterating

**Quality checklist:**
- [ ] Visual hierarchy is clear
- [ ] Interactive elements are obvious
- [ ] Copy is concise and conversational
- [ ] Spacing and alignment are pristine
- [ ] Accessibility standards met
- [ ] Edge cases handled gracefully
- [ ] Performance is acceptable

---

### Checkpoint 4: Ethical/Human Impact Review
**Your role:** Identify and mitigate potential harms before launch

**Tools:**
- **Impact mapping:** Who is affected and how?
- **Ethical review checklist:** Privacy, manipulation, accessibility, fairness
- **Consequence scanning:** What could go wrong?
- **Inclusive design review:** Test with diverse users

**AI can help with:**
- Identifying accessibility issues
- Analyzing language for bias
- Checking privacy implications

**You must do:**
- Evaluate ethical nuances AI misses
- Consider cultural context
- Challenge business assumptions
- Advocate for vulnerable users

**Questions to ask:**
- Who is excluded by this design?
- What user behaviors does this incentivize?
- How might this be misused?
- What data are we collecting and why?
- Is this design respectful of user time/attention?
- Does this create or reinforce bias?

---

### Checkpoint 5: Simplicity Edit
**Your role:** Final pass to remove everything nonessential

**Tools:**
- **Complexity audit:** Count clicks, fields, decisions required
- **Content editing:** Ruthlessly cut unnecessary copy
- **Visual reduction:** Remove decorative elements
- **Flow simplification:** Combine or eliminate steps

**AI can help with:**
- Suggesting copy edits
- Identifying unused features
- Analyzing user flows for efficiency

**You must do:**
- Make hard calls about what to cut
- Ensure simplicity doesn't sacrifice clarity
- Maintain elegance through reduction

**Simplification techniques:**
- Progressive disclosure: Show basics, hide advanced
- Smart defaults: Reduce decisions needed
- Inline help: Explain in context, not separate docs
- Remove confirmation steps (when safe)
- Combine related actions

**The test:** Can a user accomplish their goal without thinking about the interface?

## AI Orchestration Guide

### Prompt Engineering for Designers

**Structure of a good design prompt:**

1. **Context:** What problem are we solving?
2. **Constraints:** What limitations exist? (brand, technical, accessibility)
3. **Direction:** What's the strategic approach or inspiration?
4. **Deliverable:** What specific output do you need?
5. **Quality criteria:** How will you judge success?

**Example:**
```
Context: We need to redesign our manufacturing dashboard for plant managers who
check it 10+ times per day on desktop.

Constraints:
- Must work on 1920x1080 displays
- Follow CONNECT Design System
- Load under 2 seconds
- Accessible to colorblind users

Direction: Information-dense but scannable. Think Bloomberg Terminal meets
industrial design - professional, efficient, trustworthy.

Deliverable: Generate 3 layout variations for the main dashboard view showing
real-time production metrics, alerts, and daily targets.

Quality criteria:
- Most critical info visible without scrolling
- Clear visual hierarchy (alerts > current status > trends)
- Scannable in under 5 seconds
```

### AI Tools by Task

**Ideation & Variation Generation:**
- **Tools:** Midjourney, DALL-E, Claude, ChatGPT, Figma AI
- **Best for:** Visual exploration, layout variations, copy alternatives
- **Designer role:** Brief clearly, curate aggressively, combine ideas

**Research Synthesis:**
- **Tools:** Claude, ChatGPT, Dovetail, Notably
- **Best for:** Analyzing interview transcripts, finding patterns in feedback
- **Designer role:** Provide context, validate insights, identify what AI missed

**Prototype Generation:**
- **Tools:** v0, Galileo AI, Builder.io, Framer AI
- **Best for:** Quick functional prototypes from descriptions
- **Designer role:** Refine interactions, ensure quality, test with users

**Pattern Analysis:**
- **Tools:** Amplitude, Mixpanel, UserTesting AI features
- **Best for:** Identifying usage patterns, drop-off points
- **Designer role:** Interpret data, form hypotheses, design experiments

**Implementation Details:**
- **Tools:** GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code
- **Best for:** Writing component code, generating variants
- **Designer role:** Review output, ensure accessibility, maintain system

---

### Quality Curation Framework

**Not all AI output is equal. Use this filter:**

**Tier 1: Ship It** ✅
- Meets all requirements
- No obvious flaws
- Saves significant time
- → Use with minor polish

**Tier 2: Good Foundation** 🔄
- Core idea is sound
- Needs refinement
- Faster than starting from scratch
- → Iterate on it

**Tier 3: Inspiration Only** 💡
- Has one interesting element
- Overall approach wrong
- → Extract the good bit, rebuild

**Tier 4: Reject** ❌
- Misunderstood requirements
- Generic or clichéd
- Faster to start over
- → New prompt or manual approach

**Red flags for AI output:**
- Looks exactly like other AI designs you've seen
- Ignores constraints you specified
- Accessibility issues
- Cultural insensitivity
- Generic stock photo aesthetic
- Over-designed or overly complex

## Product Trio Collaboration

### The Converging Trio Model

As AI gives each role access to adjacent domains, the product trio (PM, Designer, Engineer) can become more fluid while still maintaining specialized expertise.

**Traditional boundaries:**
- PM: What to build and why
- Designer: How users experience it
- Engineer: How to build it technically

**AI-augmented reality:**
- PMs can prototype UIs to test concepts
- Designers can code working prototypes
- Engineers can generate UX variations

**The opportunity:** Faster iteration, shared understanding, less "throw it over the wall"

**The risk:** Diluted expertise, skipped steps, design-by-committee

---

### How Each Role Augments

**Product Managers gain:**
- Ability to mockup concepts before formal design
- Faster user research synthesis
- Data analysis and insight generation

**What designers must ensure:**
- PMs still involve designers early (not after prototyping)
- Mockups don't skip research and strategy
- Design quality standards are maintained

---

**Designers gain:**
- Ability to code functional prototypes
- Faster technical feasibility understanding
- Direct implementation without translation loss

**What engineers must ensure:**
- Designers understand technical constraints
- Code follows architecture patterns
- Performance and security aren't compromised

---

**Engineers gain:**
- Ability to iterate on UX details
- Understanding of design rationale
- Faster implementation of variants

**What designers must ensure:**
- Engineers don't optimize away intentional friction (e.g., delete confirmations)
- Accessibility remains priority
- Design system integrity maintained

---

### Collaboration Practices for AI Era

**1. Shared AI Briefing Sessions**
- Trio aligns on requirements together
- Everyone sees the prompts and constraints
- Reduces lost context in translation

**2. Transparent Iteration**
- Share AI-generated options across trio
- Discuss why option X over Y
- Build shared judgment criteria

**3. Defined Decision Rights**
- Clear owner for each checkpoint
- Others contribute but don't override
- Escalation path when disagreement persists

**4. Cross-Training**
- Designers learn enough code to understand tradeoffs
- Engineers learn design principles
- PMs deepen user research skills

**5. Quality Standards**
- Document what "good enough" means
- Distinguish "AI-assisted" from "AI-generated"
- Hold higher bar for user-facing work

## Citizen Development Support

### The Rise of Builder Users

**Context:** As AI makes software creation more accessible, more people will build tools for themselves. This is "citizen development" - non-engineers creating functional software.

**Designer role:** Guide, template, and protect quality even when you're not directly involved.

---

### How to Support Citizen Developers

**1. Provide Building Blocks**
- Design system components they can assemble
- Templates for common patterns
- Clear usage guidelines

**2. Establish Guardrails**
- Accessibility checklist
- Brand guidelines
- Data privacy rules
- When to involve a designer

**3. Office Hours / Support**
- Regular Q&A sessions
- Slack channel for questions
- Review process for public-facing tools

**4. Learning Resources**
- Pattern library with examples
- Tutorial videos
- "Good vs. bad" design examples
- AI prompt templates for common needs

---

### When to Intervene

**Let them build:**
- Internal tools for small teams
- Personal productivity scripts
- Quick experiments and prototypes
- Non-critical, reversible changes

**Require design review:**
- Customer-facing interfaces
- Anything collecting personal data
- Tools affecting multiple teams
- Solutions becoming permanent

**Full designer involvement:**
- Brand-sensitive touchpoints
- Compliance-critical workflows
- High-usage applications
- Anything requiring accessibility certification

---

### Citizen Development Prompt Templates

**Help citizen developers succeed with pre-written prompts:**

**Example: Internal Dashboard**
```
I need to create a dashboard for [describe users] to [describe task].

Requirements:
- Must show: [list key metrics]
- Updated: [frequency]
- Users need to: [list actions]

Generate a layout following LEGO CONNECT Design System:
- Use standard dashboard components
- Ensure WCAG AA accessibility
- Mobile-responsive
- Data updates without page refresh

Style: Professional, data-dense but scannable, enterprise application
```

**Example: Form Creation**
```
Create a form for [purpose] that collects:
- [field 1 + type]
- [field 2 + type]
- [field 3 + type]

Requirements:
- Clear field labels and help text
- Inline validation with helpful error messages
- Accessible to screen readers
- Submit button disabled until form valid
- Confirmation message after submit

Follow LEGO brand guidelines for forms.
```

## Getting Started: Your First 30 Days

### Week 1: Audit Current Work
- [ ] Review recent project: where could AI have helped?
- [ ] Identify your most time-consuming repetitive tasks
- [ ] List tasks you wish you had time for but don't

### Week 2: Experiment
- [ ] Pick one AI tool to try
- [ ] Use it on low-stakes work
- [ ] Note what works and what doesn't
- [ ] Share findings with team

### Week 3: Define Your Checkpoints
- [ ] For your typical project type, when must you intervene?
- [ ] Create checklist for each checkpoint
- [ ] Document your quality standards
- [ ] Test checkpoint model on current work

### Week 4: Establish Trio Norms
- [ ] Discuss AI usage with PM and engineer partners
- [ ] Align on who uses AI where
- [ ] Set quality expectations
- [ ] Create feedback loop for what's working

## Measuring Success

### Metrics That Matter

**Volume metrics (track but don't optimize for):**
- Designs produced per week
- Prototypes created
- Variations generated

**Quality metrics (optimize for these):**
- User satisfaction scores
- Task completion rates
- Accessibility compliance
- Time to value for users
- Design system consistency

**Impact metrics (the real goal):**
- Problems solved (not features shipped)
- User pain points eliminated
- Business outcomes achieved
- Team velocity (not just design velocity)

**Designer growth metrics:**
- Time spent on strategic vs. tactical work
- Influence on product direction
- Quality of critique and judgment
- Ability to say "no" when needed

## The Long Game

**Remember:**
- AI makes execution faster, but strategy still requires deep human thought
- The goal isn't to design more, it's to design better
- Your value isn't in making pixels, it's in making decisions
- Judgment, taste, and ethics can't be automated
- The best designers will be those who know when NOT to use AI

**Keep developing:**
- Critical thinking skills
- Ethical reasoning
- Communication and influence
- Systems thinking
- Craft and taste

**The future designer is:**
- Part strategist
- Part director
- Part curator
- Part coach
- Always an advocate for human experience
